{% trans "Entity Setup + Payroll Compliance (UF, SII, AFP, FONASA/ISAPRE)" %}

{% blocktrans trimmed %} Chile is one of Latin America's most stable and transparent economies — but compliant hiring and payroll require navigating strict labour laws, complex taxes, and local indices like UF. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Country: Chile (CL)" %} {% trans "Focus: Compliance + Payroll" %}
{% trans "Built for audit-ready operations" %}

{% blocktrans trimmed %} Translation note: this article is designed to be translated into Portuguese, Spanish, German, French, and Italian. We kept headings, lists, and key terms structured for clean localization. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "What makes Chile unique?" %} {% blocktrans trimmed %} Payroll commonly uses both CLP and UF (Unidad de Fomento), an inflation-indexed unit calculated daily. Contribution caps and tax brackets can shift with UF — making accuracy and timing critical. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Part 1: Setting Up a Business Entity in Chile" %}

{% blocktrans trimmed %} Chile has modernized incorporation significantly. The process can be faster than many neighbouring countries, but foreign companies still need careful documentation and local representation. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "1) Choose Your Legal Structure" %}

{% blocktrans trimmed %} For most international businesses, the SpA (Sociedad por Acciones) is a popular option: limited liability, 100% foreign ownership possible, and only one shareholder required. It's generally flexible and easier to manage than a traditional S.A. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "2) Register the Company" %}

{% blocktrans trimmed %} You can register through the traditional notary route or via the government portal "Tu Empresa en un Día". Incorporation typically requires Articles of Incorporation and notarization. If registering from abroad, companies often grant a legalized Power of Attorney (POA) to a local legal representative in Chile. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "3) Obtain Your Tax ID (RUT) with the SII" %}

{% blocktrans trimmed %} After registration, you must register with the Chilean Internal Revenue Service (SII — Servicio de Impuestos Internos) to obtain a RUT (Rol Único Tributario). Without a RUT, you cannot hire employees, open a bank account, or operate normally. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "4) Open a Corporate Bank Account" %}

{% blocktrans trimmed %} To pay employees locally and remit taxes, you generally need an in-country corporate bank account. This step may require the physical presence of your local legal representative depending on the bank's requirements. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "5) Secure a Municipal License (Patente Municipal)" %}

{% blocktrans trimmed %} Before commencing operations, apply for a Patente Municipal (municipal business licence) from the municipality where your legal address is registered. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Part 2: Navigating Payroll & Employment Laws in Chile" %}

{% blocktrans trimmed %} Payroll is where complexity accelerates. Many statutory caps and tax brackets are tied to UF — so the calculations must be kept current and consistently applied each pay cycle. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "1) Mandatory Employer Contributions" %}

{% blocktrans trimmed %} Employers typically pay statutory contributions in addition to gross salary (often around 4%–5%, depending on factors like industry risk). {% endblocktrans %}

  • {% trans "Unemployment Insurance (AFC)" %}

    {% blocktrans trimmed %} Employers contribute 2.4% for permanent employees (and 3% for fixed-term contracts). {% endblocktrans %}

  • {% trans "Survivor & Disability Insurance (SIS)" %}

    {% blocktrans trimmed %} Typically around 1.5% (rates can vary based on applicable rules and providers). {% endblocktrans %}

  • {% trans "Work Accident & Disease Insurance (Mutual)" %}

    {% blocktrans trimmed %} A baseline of 0.93% plus a risk-based premium up to ~3.4% depending on your industry. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "2) Employee Deductions (Withholdings)" %}

{% blocktrans trimmed %} Employers must withhold and remit the employee's share of social security (often ~17.6% of gross pay) and monthly income tax. {% endblocktrans %}

  • {% trans "Pension Fund (AFP)" %}

    {% blocktrans trimmed %} 10% paid to the private pension administrator chosen by the employee. {% endblocktrans %}

  • {% trans "Health Insurance (FONASA or ISAPRE)" %}

    {% blocktrans trimmed %} 7% paid to either the public system (FONASA) or a private insurer (ISAPRE). {% endblocktrans %}

  • {% trans "Unemployment Insurance (AFC)" %}

    {% blocktrans trimmed %} 0.6% for permanent employees (employee portion). {% endblocktrans %}

  • {% trans "Income Tax" %}

    {% blocktrans trimmed %} Progressive monthly tax from 0% up to 40%, determined by UF-linked tax brackets. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "3) Legal Gratification (Profit Sharing)" %}

{% blocktrans trimmed %} Under the Chilean Labour Code, profitable companies must share 30% of net income with employees, or commonly choose a guaranteed bonus of 25% of base salary (capped at 4.75× the minimum wage) paid monthly to satisfy the requirement. {% endblocktrans %}

{% trans "Part 3: The Danger of Vendor Bloat" %}

{% blocktrans trimmed %} Many companies start with a local accounting firm — but expansion across LATAM can create a fragmented vendor stack: PDFs, spreadsheets, different currencies, and inconsistent definitions. In Chile, small UF-related miscalculations or late filings can trigger immediate and compounding penalties. {% endblocktrans %}
